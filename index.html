Academic paper- Confidence in the police International students’ perspective on police in Sweden and in their home countries - a quantitative study in variables that affect confidence

Authors: Andersson Stridh, Eder, Hsu, Klamvik, Persson

Abstract

This study aims to investigate whether variables such as gender, ethnicity, nationality, neighborhood of residence and previous encounters with the police affect the level of trust international students have towards the Swedish police and police in their home country. Furthermore, it also examines the discrepancy (or lack thereof) in the confidence that international students have in the Swedish police and the police in their home countries. Studies show that confidence in the police is the main attribute for successful communication between officers and civilians, as well as for compliance and cooperation. To examine this, a quantitative survey was conducted where a total of 104 international students in Sweden participated in the survey. The results revealed that gender, victimization experience, and negative encounters with the police harm confidence in the police. On the other hand, previous encounters with the police and ethnicity did not have a significant effect. Overall, it could be determined that international students have more confidence in the Swedish police than in the police of their home countries. This relationship is complex and appears to vary greatly depending on the person’s background.

National Culture affecting Police Trust? A quantitative study among international students in Sweden based on Hofstede's Power Distance Index

Authors: Andersson Stridh, Eder, Hsu, Klamvik, Persson

Abstract

The aim of this research is to find out if there is a difference in the degree of trust towards the police between students from cultures with high Power Distance and students from cultures with low Power Distance. Globalization has changed the world, intercultural skills are increasingly in demand in companies as well as in private life. This is why many students are drawn to study abroad, in order to develop their intercultural skills. As a base for this paper, primarily Gert Hofstede's Power Distance Index was used. The question is important, on the one hand, because dealing with different cultures is increasingly important and, on the other hand, because trust in the police is an important prerequisite for police performance and efficiency. In order to find an answer to the question, a quantitative survey was conducted. A total of 104 international students in Sweden participated in the survey. The results showed that there is little evidence suggesting that coming from a country with a high or low power distance has an effect on trust in the police. Moreover, it could be found that women on average have more trust in the police and that there is a complex relationship between ethnicity and trust in the police. Thus, continuous intercultural training is recommended.

Academic paper- Language usage in Swedish print advertising: An analysis of English and Swedish code-switching norms

Author: T. Klamvik

Abstract

Code-switching is a term used to describe a behavior where a discourse participant switches between two or more languages. Although code-switching can be used in written text, there is little research on its use outside of spoken discourse. This study explores how English code-switching is currently used in Swedish print advertising. Furthermore, the study uses theoretical models to categorize different types of code-switching to see which ones are used most frequently. Code-switching is commonly used only informally between two or more discourse participants, but this study tries to assess how it might be applied by copywriters to affect consumer persuasion. The results confirm previous studies in demonstrating that English is used considerably in Swedish advertising. However, it also highlights how English usage can vary greatly depending on advertisement layout and sentence construction.
